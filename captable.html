Network Structure — Flagship & Satellites

▾
Osteoid Centers scales as a hub-and-spoke network, not a single company opening branches. This explains how ownership, licensing, and cash flow work as new cities come online — and why the flagship's cap table (this page) doesn't change shape as the network grows.
1 — The Istanbul Clinic Is the Flagship
Istanbul does two jobs at once: it runs its own clinic (the business modeled everywhere else in this dashboard), and it acts as the management company for the whole network — the entity that licenses the technology onward, collects management fees, and holds equity in each new city.
2 — Satellites Are Separate Companies
Izmir, Ankara, and every future city are each their own legal company — majority-owned by the flagship, but with their own local investors and their own regulatory license (a standalone ısmarlama ortez-protez merkezi permit, with its own sorumlu müdür). A satellite is not a branch; it's a separately licensed clinic that happens to run on Osteoid's technology and processes.

4 — The Management Fee Is the Engine
Each satellite pays the flagship a management fee for administration, coordination, and marketing. That fee is charged on the satellite's gross revenue, before any profit split — so the flagship earns it on 100% of satellite revenue, but bears its cost only pro-rata to its own equity stake in what's left. That asymmetry — full fee income, partial fee cost — is the economic engine of the network model, not an incidental detail. See the live management-fee and ownership sliders on the Multi-Year Plan page.
5 — Growth Doesn't Touch the Flagship Cap Table
Satellite expansion is funded by local investors at the satellite level — not by raising more money into the flagship. So when Izmir or Ankara opens, the ownership table on this page does not change shape or dilute. The flagship investor's exposure to network growth comes entirely through two channels that already flow into the Contribution Register on the Investor & Agreement page: the management fee stream, and the flagship's equity stake in each satellite's profit.

Why This Venture Is De-Risked

▾
This isn't a first-time startup bet — it's a proven platform entering a new venue. The points below are why the DCF on the Investor & Agreement page prices the discount rate at the proven-technology rate (currently —, r = —), not the startup rate.
Proven at International Scale
The Turnkey/Bracesys platform is already operational in 17 countries through the KiMedix distribution network, with 3,000+ braces produced on a CE-marked platform. This clinic is not the first deployment of untested technology — it's the first flagship deployment of a technology that already works at scale elsewhere.
Exclusive Territorial Rights
The flagship holds master development rights for its territory — currently valued at — in the Contribution Register — including the right to sub-license satellite clinics elsewhere in the territory (see Network Structure above). This isn't a shared market; it's a granted exclusive.
No Local Substitute
Local competitors cannot access or replicate the underlying CAD/CAM design library and production process — currently valued at — in the Contribution Register. It's proprietary, patent-backed (Bracesys) IP, not an off-the-shelf capability a new entrant could buy or copy.
Pre-Validated Workflows
Clinical and production workflows arrive pre-validated from the existing international deployments, rather than being built from scratch — the leading clinicians on the team have already run this process, not just designed it on paper. Remaining execution risk is single-site (this one clinic, this one city), not technology risk or market-fit risk.
